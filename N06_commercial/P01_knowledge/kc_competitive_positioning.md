# Competitive Positioning Methods — Universal Frameworks

## 1. Ries & Trout Positioning Statement

Developed by Al Ries and Jack Trout in *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981). Still the gold standard.

### Template
```
For [TARGET SEGMENT] who [UNMET NEED OR PROBLEM],
[BRAND NAME] is the [CATEGORY/FRAME OF REFERENCE]
that [KEY BENEFIT / POINT OF DIFFERENCE]
because [REASON TO BELIEVE / PROOF POINT].
```

### Example (Slack)
```
For work teams who suffer from fragmented email and excessive meetings,
Slack is the corporate communication platform
that centralizes conversations, files, and integrations in one place
because it connects 2,400+ apps and searches all institutional history.
```

### Rules
- **TARGET**: specific, not "everyone"
- **NEED**: the problem they have, not what you do
- **CATEGORY**: where you compete (establishes context)
- **BENEFIT**: single most compelling differentiator
- **RTB**: verifiable proof, not just a claim

## 2. Blue Ocean Strategy — ERRC Grid

Source: Kim & Mauborgne, *Blue Ocean Strategy* (2004). Reconstructs market boundaries by questioning industry assumptions.

### Red Ocean vs Blue Ocean

| Dimension | Red Ocean | Blue Ocean |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Focus | Beat competition | Make competition irrelevant |
| Demand | Existing market | New demand creation |
| Value-Cost | Trade-off | Simultaneous differentiation + low cost |
| Strategy | Choose differentiation OR low cost | Value innovation = both |

### ERRC Grid (Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create)

```
┌─────────────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────────┐
│ ELIMINATE                       │ RAISE                           │
│ What factors does the industry  │ What factors should be raised   │
│ take for granted that should    │ well above the industry         │
│ be eliminated?                  │ standard?                       │
├─────────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────────┤
│ REDUCE                          │ CREATE                          │
│ What factors should be reduced  │ What factors should be created  │
│ well below the industry         │ that the industry has never     │
│ standard?                       │ offered?                        │
└─────────────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────────┘
```

### How to Apply
1. Map all competing factors in your industry
2. For each factor, place it in E, R, R, or C
3. The ERRC choices define your **new value curve**
4. Test: Does your curve diverge from competitors? Does it have a compelling tagline?

### Classic Example — Cirque du Soleil
- **Eliminate**: Star performers, animal shows, aisle concessions
- **Reduce**: Fun/humor, thrill/danger
- **Raise**: Unique venue, artistic music/dance
- **Create**: Theme, refined environment, multiple productions, artistic concept

## 3. Porter's Generic Strategies

Michael Porter, *Competitive Strategy* (1980). Three defensible positions in any industry.

```
                    COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE
                 Low Cost    |   Differentiation
             ────────────────┼────────────────────
BROAD        Cost            │   Differentiation
TARGET       Leadership      │
             ────────────────┼────────────────────
NARROW       Cost Focus      │   Differentiation
TARGET                       │   Focus
```

### Descriptions

**Cost Leadership**
- Lowest cost producer in the industry
- Does NOT mean lowest price (can be sold at average price for higher profit)
- Requires: economies of scale, proprietary technology, preferential access to raw materials
- Risk: technology change, imitation, inflation erodes cost advantage

**Differentiation**
- Unique on dimensions widely valued by buyers
- Can command premium price
- Sources: brand, design, technology, customer service, distribution network
- Risk: cost gap too large, uniqueness no longer valued, imitation

**Focus (Cost or Differentiation)**
- Narrow competitive scope within an industry
- Serves a particular buyer group, segment, or geographic market
- Outperforms broader competitors within the chosen segment
- Risk: the target segment becomes unattractive or is absorbed by a broader competitor

### The Stuck-in-the-Middle Trap
Companies that try to pursue multiple strategies simultaneously risk being "stuck in the middle" — performing poorly on all dimensions. Commit to one.

## 4. Value Proposition Canvas (Strategyzer)

Alex Osterwalder et al. Maps the fit between what you offer and what the customer needs.

### Customer Profile (right side)
```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              CUSTOMER PROFILE            │
│                                          │
│  CUSTOMER JOBS                           │
│  What is the customer trying to get      │
│  done? (functional, social, emotional)   │
│                                          │
│  PAINS                                   │
│  What annoys them before, during,        │
│  or after getting the job done?          │
│  Risks they fear?                        │
│                                          │
│  GAINS                                   │
│  Outcomes and benefits they want:        │
│  required, expected, desired, unexpected │
└──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Value Map (left side)
```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│               VALUE MAP                  │
│                                          │
│  PRODUCTS & SERVICES                     │
│  What you offer (list of products/       │
│  services/features)                      │
│                                          │
│  PAIN RELIEVERS                          │
│  How your offering alleviates specific   │
│  customer pains                          │
│                                          │
│  GAIN CREATORS                           │
│  How your offering creates specific      │
│  customer gains                          │
└──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Fit
**Problem-Solution Fit**: Pain relievers address real pains. Gain creators deliver desired gains.
**Product-Market Fit**: Customers act (buy, use, recommend).

## 5. Perceptual Mapping

A 2D competitive grid that visualizes where brands sit relative to each other on dimensions customers care about.

### How to Build
1. **Choose 2 axes** that matter to your target customer (e.g., Price vs Quality; Traditional vs Innovative; Simple vs Feature-rich)
2. **Plot all competitors** based on customer perception (use surveys if possible)
3. **Identify white spaces** — areas with no current competitor
4. **Choose your position** — ideally in a white space near a cluster of your target customers

### Axis Candidates
- Price (low ↔ high)
- Quality (low ↔ high)
- Accessibility (niche ↔ mass market)
- Formality (casual ↔ professional)
- Innovation (traditional ↔ cutting-edge)
- Speed (slow ↔ fast)
- Complexity (simple ↔ sophisticated)

### Warning
Perceptual maps show *current perception*, not necessarily reality. Position is owned in the customer's mind, not in your product spec.

## 6. Category Design

Play Bigger (2016). Rather than competing in an existing category, *define a new one*.

### When to Create a Category
- Your product does something genuinely new that existing categories don't capture
- Existing category language puts you in a losing comparison (you'll always be compared to the leader)
- You can articulate a problem that nobody has named yet

### Category Design Steps
1. **Name the problem** — what is the new category of problem?
2. **Name the category** — what is the new category of solution?
3. **Define the category** — educate the market on why the problem matters
4. **Own the category** — become the reference point (category king)

### Examples
- Salesforce didn't sell "CRM software" — they created "cloud CRM"
- Uber didn't sell "taxi" — they created "ride-sharing"
- Slack didn't sell "corporate chat" — they created "team collaboration platform"

### Category vs Product Positioning
```
Product Positioning: "We are the best [EXISTING THING]"
Category Design:     "We are the [NEW THING] — and here's why you need it"
```

## 7. Differentiation Statement

The shortest expression of your competitive positioning.

### Template
```
We are the only [CATEGORY] that [UNIQUE DIFFERENTIATOR].
```

### Examples
```
"We are the only CRM that sends personalized follow-ups automatically."
"We are the only fitness app that adapts to your stress levels in real time."
"We are the only agency that guarantees first-page ranking in 90 days."
```

### Test
- Can any competitor say the exact same thing? → Not differentiated
- Would your target customer care about this? → If not, choose another differentiator
- Is it believable? → Must have proof

## 8. Competitive Audit Template

Use to map the competitive landscape before positioning.

| Competitor | Category | Price Point | Core Differentiator | Target Segment | Strengths | Weaknesses | Positioning Claim |
|------------|----------|-------------|---------------------|---------------|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| [Name]     | [Cat]    | [$$]        | [What makes them unique] | [Who they serve] | [+] | [-] | [Their tagline/statement] |

### Dimensions to Audit
- **Positioning**: What do they claim? Who do they target?
- **Pricing**: Freemium, subscription, one-time, premium?
- **Distribution**: Direct, marketplace, partner network?
- **Brand**: Tone, visual identity, archetype
- **Content**: What topics do they own?
- **Social proof**: Reviews, case studies, certifications

## 9. Brand Positioning Scorecard

Rate your positioning on 4 dimensions (1-10 each):

| Dimension | Definition | Score (1-10) |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| **Uniqueness** | How different is your position from all competitors? | — |
| **Credibility** | Can you actually deliver on the claim? | — |
| **Relevance** | Does your target segment care deeply about this differentiator? | — |
| **Sustainability** | How hard is it for competitors to copy this position? | — |

**Minimum viable position**: All scores >= 6. Total >= 28 = strong positioning.

## 10. Repositioning

When and how to shift your competitive position.

### When to Reposition
- Your current position is occupied by a stronger competitor
- Your target segment has shifted in preferences
- A new technology makes your current differentiator irrelevant
- You've outgrown your original niche

### Repositioning Approaches
1. **Value repositioning**: Change the benefit you emphasize (same product, new frame)
2. **Competitive repositioning**: Explicitly contrast with the competitor ("Unlike X, we...")
3. **Segment repositioning**: Same product, new target audience
4. **Category repositioning**: Reframe what category you belong to

### Risks
- Alienating existing customers
- Credibility gap (new claim doesn't match product reality)
- Confusion if transition is too abrupt

## 11. Common Positioning Mistakes

| Mistake | Description | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| **Underpositioning** | Too vague to be memorable ("We deliver quality and value") | Commit to one specific differentiator |
| **Overpositioning** | Too narrow, excludes potential customers | Widen to adjacent use cases |
| **Confused positioning** | Multiple conflicting claims | Choose one primary positioning |
| **Copying the leader** | "We're like [Leader] but better" | Create distance — find a different axis |
| **Feature positioning** | Leading with product features not customer benefits | Translate features into outcomes |
| **Aspirational positioning** | Claiming something you can't yet deliver | Earn the claim first, then make it |
| **Me-too positioning** | Looks like every competitor | Map perceptual space, find white space |
